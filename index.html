Data Cleaning

The devil's in the details—especially when those details aren't consistent. Digging into the dataset revealed a classic data problem: different teams had entered the same information in different ways.

"Low fat," "LF," and "low fat" all meant the same thing, but the database saw them as entirely different categories.

UPDATE quickcart_data
SET Item_Fat_Content =
CASE
    WHEN Item_Fat_Content IN ('LF', 'low fat') THEN 'Low Fat'
    WHEN Item_Fat_Content = 'reg' THEN 'Regular'
    ELSE Item_Fat_Content
END;

Key Insights Discovered

Sales Performance by Fat Content

In an increasingly health-conscious market, understanding how nutritional profiles affect sales isn't just interesting—it's essential.

SELECT 
    Item_Fat_Content, 
    ROUND(SUM(Total_Sales), 2) AS Total_Sales,
    ROUND(AVG(Rating), 2) AS Average_rating,
    COUNT(*) AS No_of_Order
FROM 
    quickcart_data 
GROUP BY Item_Fat_Content
ORDER BY Total_Sales DESC;

The results revealed a decisive consumer preference:

  • Low Fat products generated $776,319.68 in sales (64.6% of total revenue)
  • Regular products contributed $425,361.80 (35.4% of total revenue)
  • Both categories maintain nearly identical average ratings of 3.97
  • Low Fat products recorded almost twice the number of orders (5,517 vs 3,006)

This data challenged conventional wisdom at Quick Cart. While they had been expanding their "Regular" fat content offerings based on taste-test feedback, customers were clearly voting with their wallets for healthier options.

Location and Product Type Analysis

Retail success isn't just about what you sell—it's about matching products to place.

SELECT Outlet_Location_Type, 
       ISNULL([Low Fat], 0) AS Low_Fat, 
       ISNULL([Regular], 0) AS Regular
FROM 
(
    SELECT Outlet_Location_Type, Item_Fat_Content, 
           CAST(SUM(Total_Sales) AS DECIMAL(10,2)) AS Total_Sales
    FROM quickcart_data
    GROUP BY Outlet_Location_Type, Item_Fat_Content
) AS SourceTable
PIVOT 
(
    SUM(Total_Sales) 
    FOR Item_Fat_Content IN ([Low Fat], [Regular])
) AS PivotTable
ORDER BY Outlet_Location_Type;

This PIVOT query transformed complex relationships into actionable insights:

  • Tier 3 locations show the strongest performance for both categories:
    • $306,806.99 in Low Fat sales
    • $165,326.03 in Regular sales
  • All location tiers show stronger preference for Low Fat products
  • Tier 1 locations show the smallest gap between Low Fat and Regular product preferences

The patterns were striking—urban locations showed dramatically different preferences than suburban ones.

Store Size Impact on Sales

In retail, square footage equals investment. Understanding the return on that investment shapes everything from leasing decisions to store design.

SELECT
    Outlet_Size,
    ROUND(SUM(Total_Sales), 2) AS Total_Sales,
    ROUND(100.0 * SUM(Total_Sales) / 
        SUM(SUM(Total_Sales)) OVER(), 2) AS Sales_Percentage
FROM quickcart_data
GROUP BY Outlet_Size
ORDER BY Total_Sales DESC;

The analysis revealed surprising efficiency patterns:

  • Medium-sized stores generate 42.27% of total sales ($507,895.73)
  • Small stores contribute 37.01% ($444,794.17)
  • Large stores account for only 20.72% ($249,991.58)

The window function here did more than calculate percentages—it revealed efficiency. Medium-sized stores weren't just generating strong total sales—they were delivering more revenue per square foot than their larger counterparts.

Comprehensive Outlet Type Analysis

Different store formats serve different needs. Understanding which formats drive both volume and satisfaction helps optimize the store portfolio.

SELECT 
    Outlet_Type,
    ROUND(SUM(Total_Sales), 2) AS Total_sales,
    ROUND(100.0 * SUM(Total_Sales) / 
        SUM(SUM(Total_Sales)) OVER(), 2) AS Sales_Percentage,
    ROUND(AVG(Total_Sales), 2) AS Avg_sales,
    ROUND(AVG(Rating), 2) AS Average_rating,
    COUNT(*) AS No_of_Order
FROM
    quickcart_data
GROUP BY Outlet_Type
ORDER BY Total_sales DESC;

This comprehensive analysis revealed:

  • Supermarket Type1 dominates with 65.54% of total sales ($787,549.89)
  • Supermarket Type1 also has the highest customer satisfaction with an average rating of 3.96
  • Supermarket Type3 has the highest average transaction value but lowest total sales
  • Grocery Stores contribute 12.64% of sales with relatively high average ratings of 3.99

This holistic view connected dots that had previously been examined in isolation. Supermarket Type1 stores weren't just revenue leaders—they also enjoyed above-average customer ratings despite having lower average transaction values.

Business Recommendations

Product Strategy

1. Accelerate Low Fat product launches – The data clearly shows a 2:1 consumer preference for Low Fat options, despite equal satisfaction ratings.

2. Restructure pricing strategy – Despite Supermarket Type1 stores having high volume, they have the lowest average transaction value. Consider bundle deals to increase basket size.

3. Optimize product assortment – Focus on the 20% of products that drive 80% of sales, while maintaining enough variety to satisfy the consumer desire for choice.

Implementation difficulty:
Medium

Location Strategy

1. Focus expansion on Tier 3 locations – These areas show the strongest performance for both product types, suggesting untapped potential.

2. Tailor product mix by location – Tier 1 locations show nearly equal preference for Regular and Low Fat products, while Tier 3 areas strongly favor Low Fat options.

3. Pilot new store formats in Tier 2 locations – These areas show moderate performance for both categories, making them ideal testing grounds for new concepts.

Implementation difficulty:
High

Store Format Optimization

1. Prioritize medium-sized stores – These locations deliver the highest revenue per square foot, suggesting an optimal balance between variety and operating costs.

2. Reconsider large format stores – Despite their size, these locations deliver only 20.72% of total sales, indicating potential inefficiencies.

3. Leverage grocery store loyalty – Despite their smaller footprint, these formats maintain high customer satisfaction ratings, suggesting a stronger community connection.

Implementation difficulty:
Medium

Customer Experience

1. Implement targeted loyalty programs – Different consumer segments clearly value different attributes; tailor rewards accordingly.

2. Enhance nutritional transparency – The strong preference for Low Fat products indicates consumers value health information; make it easier to find.

3. Create store-specific promotions – Customer behaviors vary dramatically by location type; one-size-fits-all promotions waste marketing dollars.

Implementation difficulty:
Low
